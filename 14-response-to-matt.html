14  Response to Your Comments

14.1 Closing the loop

This book opened with your five comments and a promise to answer each one from four angles — mathematics, code, a known-truth simulation, and PISA itself. Here is the ledger, closed.

C2 How do we get \(T_{\text{MI}}\) without using A or B methods? · C4 I don’t understand where this comes from? isn’t it model specific and needs … estimating multiple models?

You were right — and the saving is narrower than the proposal made it sound. \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is model-specific and it does take many fits. But they are \(M(R{+}1)=810\) fast lme4 REML refits, not MCMC runs, assembled by a pipeline-independent BRR-Fay subroutine that all three pipelines share (Section 5.1). In code it is one function, assemble_pisa_brr_fay_target (Section 8.1); on the real PISA data it ran in about ten seconds per country (Section 13.1). Pipeline C does not get the variance target “without A or B” in the sense of getting it for free — it gets it from the same cheap machinery everyone uses, and its only economy is the MCMC count, ten down to one. And the target is not just algebra: in a known-truth simulation with informative, unequal-probability sampling, the BRR-Fay \(T_{\text{MI}}\) delivers nominal interval coverage where ignoring the design collapses to \(0.07\) (Section 10.8) — so it is design-valid, not merely model-valid. (That check stresses within-school informative selection; informative school selection — where the exact two-stage weights bite hardest — is the natural next stress-test, flagged rather than claimed.)

C3 Isn’t \((1+1/M)B\) for the plausible values \(y\) not the model parameters (betas)? How do we connect this to the variance of the parameters?

It is a variance of the betas, not of \(y\). Each plausible value is pushed through the GLS map \(\hat\beta_m=(X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega\,y^{(m)}\), and \(B\) is the covariance of those ten coefficient vectors in parameter space — the missing arrow, drawn explicitly via the sandwich \(\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_m)=F\operatorname{Cov}(y^{(m)})F^\top\) (Section 4.1). The variability originates in \(y\) but is measured in \(\beta\).

C5 Fixing the variance components? why not do importance resampling for the joint distribution instead of the conditional/profile version?

Two answers, because it is two questions. Why only the betas: the licensing identity is exact only for the fixed effects (their GLS gradient is linear in \(y\)), there is no design-based target for the variance components in this paper, and an affine map would violate their positivity — so calibrating them is unsafe, not merely unmotivated (Section 6.1). And the simulation made the boundary visible: the fixed-effect identity held to \(10^{-14}\) while the variance-component “identity” drifted \(4.7\%\) at the pilot scale and \(0.7\%\) at PISA scale (Section 10.1). Why not joint resampling: that is Pipeline B’s PSIS for the \(\beta\) block (Section 7.1) — joint variance-component resampling walks straight into the heavy-tail problem you flag in C1, which is why we keep it for the follow-up paper.

C1 … for importance sampling we may want to further fit a second time and further temper … Should double check the pareto version of importance sampling.

We built your ideas and ran them. The Pareto \(\hat k\) gate is in place; the tempering exponent \(1/(kM)\) is a real lever; the two-MCMC rescue is specified (Section 7.1). The experiment of Section 11.1 ran \(1/M\) vs \(1/2M\) vs \(1/3M\) on known-truth data: at \(1/M\) the worst \(\hat k\) was \(0.25\) (already reliable), tempering pushed it lower still (your mechanism, confirmed), and the point estimate barely moved — vindicating your instinct to run \(1/M\) first. On the real PISA data the worst \(\hat k\) was \(0.07\) (USA) and \(0.04\) (Korea) (Section 13.1): the importance sampling is stable there, and Pipeline B’s point tracks A and C-Direct. Its standard errors, though, run a little below the orthodox target — most for the between-school slope — because B reweights a single shared draw cloud; a small \(\hat k\) certifies that the sampler is stable, not that B’s multiple-imputation variance equals A’s. So C-Direct is the reported deliverable, with B the diagnostic alongside it, and the tempering and second-fit levers in reserve for harder data.

And the three results that, together, are the whole argument:

  • Theorem 2.2 is exact: the stacked GLS solution equalled the Rubin estimate to \(10^{-14}\) (Section 10.1).
  • Pipeline C’s point and uncertainty are Pipeline A’s, exactly: centered at the external \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) and calibrated to the same \(T_{\text{MI}}\), C-Direct reproduces A’s point (\(\max|\hat\beta_C-\hat\beta_A|=0\)) and its standard errors (\(\max|\text{SE}_C-\text{SE}_A|\) is \(0\) to floating point, \(\le 3\times10^{-15}\) on PISA) — in simulation and on real data.
  • It holds on the real data: USA and Korea, the three pipelines agree in point — C-Direct reproduces A’s intervals exactly, while B’s run narrower (by up to a third for the between-school slope) — at \(\hat k=0.04\)–\(0.07\), with one MCMC instead of ten.

14.3 Where I’d like to go next

A few things I’d value your steer on:

  1. The tempering follow-up. We now have a first reproduction of the case you sketched: at a simulated PISA scale (\(J=150\)) the \(1/M\) bridge’s worst \(\hat k\) climbs to \(0.83\), past the gate, and Pipeline B fails safe to C-Direct (Section 10.1) — the large-\(n\), separated per-PV “islands” you anticipated, exhibited rather than asserted. I’d like to develop it into a fuller study with the second-fit rescue earning its keep. This feels like a paper-worthy methodological contribution in its own right.
  2. The exact two-stage weighting. The substantive PISA story (the between-school gap under exact school-times-conditional-student weights) sits alongside this methodology and is where the empirical headline lives; I’d like to align the pipeline machinery with that weighting next.
  3. Overleaf. Thank you for offering to host — yes, let’s. I’ll port the manuscript skeleton there so the comments and edits flow both ways.

Thank you for comments precise enough to organize a whole book around. If any answer here still doesn’t land, that is the most useful thing you could tell me.

— JoonHo
