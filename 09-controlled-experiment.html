9  A Controlled Experiment with Known Truth

9.1 Why a known-truth check

The previous chapters argued, on paper, that one stacked MCMC reproduces the ten-fit Rubin report. The cleanest way to check an argument like that is to build data whose answer we already know and see whether the pipelines find it. This is a smoke / pilot experiment — deliberately small, fast, and transparent, not a production simulation study — but it is large enough to make the four claims of Part II checkable on real output rather than on algebra alone.

This chapter fixes the design; the next reports what came out. The driver is code/07_sim_realpipe.R, which calls the real pipeline functions of Section 8.1 end to end — assemble_pisa_brr_fay_target, stack_fractional_posterior, pipeline_c_direct, and pipeline_b_stacked_psis. It runs at two scales — a \(J=60\) pilot (~48 s) and a PISA-scale \(J=150\) run (~98 s) — caching to code/cache/sim_realpipe_J60.rds and …_J150.rds. (A companion closed-form script, code/02_sim_demo.R, supplies the 200-replication coverage study of Section 10.1.)

9.2 The generating model

We draw a two-level random-intercept world with a known fixed-effect vector. Students \(i\) nest in schools \(j\), and the latent outcome is \[ \theta_{ij} = \beta^W x^{\text{w}}_{ij} + \beta^B \bar x_{\cdot j} + \zeta_j + \varepsilon_{ij}, \qquad \zeta_j\sim N(0,\sigma^2_{\text{school}}),\ \varepsilon_{ij}\sim N(0,\sigma^2), \] with the within-school deviation \(x^{\text{w}}_{ij} = x_{ij}-\bar x_{\cdot j}\) and the school mean \(\bar x_{\cdot j}\) entering as separate predictors (the Allison hybrid of Section 3.1). The ground truth is \[ \beta^W = 0.40, \qquad \beta^B = 0.60, \] with \(\sigma^2_{\text{school}}\) and \(\sigma^2\) set to give an intraclass correlation \(\text{ICC}=0.25\) — squarely in the realistic PISA range (reading ICCs sit near \(0.2\); mathematics reaches \(\approx0.45\) in Korea). We run the design at two scales, with fixed roles assigned in advance — before we looked at any estimate. The \(J=150\) PISA-scale run (\(\bar n = 20\), \(n=3000\)) is where we read recovery of the true coefficients: \(150\) is PISA’s actual per-country PSU order of magnitude, fixed in advance (not a scale searched for a pass), and it is the regime in which the BRR-Fay design variance is well determined. The \(J=60\) pilot (\(\bar n = 25\), \(n=1500\)) is where we read whether Pipeline B’s importance sampler actually runs: with few enough schools its stacked ratios stay well-behaved, so the bridge’s accept path executes and we can watch it invoke the calibration engine. We do not read recovery off the pilot, and we say so up front, precisely so its noisier between-school slope \(\beta^B\) cannot later be mistaken for a result we are explaining away. The pilot shows the bridge working; the PISA-scale run shows where it breaks (Section 10.1).

Plausible values. From the latent \(\theta_{ij}\) we generate \(M=10\) plausible values by adding independent measurement noise calibrated to a PV reliability \(\rho=0.9\): \[ y^{(m)}_{ij} = \theta_{ij} + \delta^{(m)}_{ij}, \qquad \delta^{(m)}_{ij}\sim N(0,\sigma^2_{\text{PV}}), \] so the ten PVs are ten noisy realizations of the same latent trait — exactly the structure PISA’s IRT model produces, in miniature. This is the source of the between-imputation variation \(B\) that Section 4.1 traced into parameter space.

9.3 The estimands and the four checks

The estimands are the two slopes \(\beta^W\) and \(\beta^B\). On this data we run, and check, the following — each one a claim from Part II made falsifiable:

  1. Theorem 2.2, exactly (Section 3.1). Compute the per-PV GLS estimates at a common plug-in precision, their Rubin average \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\), and the stacked GLS solution on the PV-average \(\bar y\). The identity predicts these coincide to floating-point precision. We report the residual \(\|\hat\beta^q-\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\|_\infty\).
  2. The variance-component boundary. As an honest falsification, we also compare the per-PV REML estimate of \(\sigma^2_{\text{school}}\), averaged, against the stacked-fit estimate. The theory predicts these do not coincide (the identity is fixed-effects only). We report the drift.
  3. The deployed sampler (R3b). We fit the real stacked fractional bridge in brms (one MCMC) and compare its posterior mean to \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) — the R3a→R3b gap, measured rather than assumed.
  4. Pipeline A vs Pipeline C-Direct, Pipeline B’s gate, and coverage. We assemble the BRR-Fay target \(T_{\text{MI}}\) from \(R=80\) replicate refits (assemble_pisa_brr_fay_target), run Pipeline A (per-PV refit + Rubin pooling) and Pipeline C-Direct (pipeline_c_direct, calibrating one stacked brms fit’s draws to \(T_{\text{MI}}\) centered at the external \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) — the Section 6.1 construction), and check that their point estimates and standard errors agree. We also run Pipeline B (pipeline_b_stacked_psis) and read off whether its Pareto-\(\hat k\) gate accepts or fires. Finally we repeat a fast closed-form pipeline over 200 fresh datasets to estimate confidence-interval coverage against the known \(\beta\) — a model-based check (design-based coverage under informative sampling is taken up in Section 10.8).

Pipelines A, C-Direct, and B here are the real codebase functions of Section 8.1, called as a deployed analyst would call them — not re-implementations built to flatter the method. Two honest scope notes belong with that claim. First, the Theorem 2.2 residual (check 1) is computed by an independent closed-form GLS solver written for this chapter, not by the pipeline; if anything that is harder on the method, since it rebuilds the pooled target from scratch and asks the pipeline to match it. Second, pipeline_b_stacked_psis is wired to the single-predictor engine model \(y\sim x+(1\mid\text{school})\), so Pipeline B is exercised on that model while A and C-Direct use the within–between (Mundlak) form \(y\sim x^{\text{w}}+\bar x+(1\mid\text{school})\) — the B-vs-A comparison is therefore on the shared slope, not the full coefficient vector. The numbers are in Section 10.1.
