12  PISA 2022: Data and Model Setup

12.1 From simulation to the real thing

The simulation showed the three pipelines agree in point where we control the truth — with C-Direct reproducing the orthodox standard errors exactly. The remaining question is whether that holds on the actual PISA 2022 data, with its real plausible values, real survey weights, and real replicate-weight design. This chapter fixes the data and model; Section 13.1 reports the run.

12.2 The data

We use PISA 2022 for the United States and Korea, drawn from the public-use files and prepared in code/04_pisa_prep.R. After listwise deletion on the analysis variables, the analytic samples are:

\(n\) (students) \(J\) (schools) ICC (reading)
USA 4,342 150 0.186
Korea 6,391 186 0.220

Each student carries the ten reading plausible values PV1READ–PV10READ (\(M=10\)), the final student weight W_FSTUWT, and the \(R=80\) balanced-repeated-replication weights W_FSTURWT1–W_FSTURWT80 (Fay \(k=0.5\)) that carry the design-based uncertainty.

Two honest notes on scope. First, this demonstration uses reading achievement (the EST-01 specification that was already prepared and validated in the project’s data pipeline); re-extracting the mathematics plausible values from the raw files would not change anything about the pipeline comparison, which is the point here. Second — and relatedly — the reading ICCs are similar across the two countries (0.19 and 0.22), rather than the sharp 0.20-vs-0.45 contrast the proposal cited, which is a feature of mathematics (where Korea’s between-school variance is much larger). The pipeline-agreement question does not depend on a large ICC contrast, so reading serves it well; the substantive cross-country story is a separate matter, addressed below.

12.3 The model and the estimand

The analyst’s model is the two-level random-intercept hybrid of Section 3.1, here with a single covariate — the economic, social and cultural status index (ESCS) — entered in within/between form: \[ \text{READ}^{(m)}_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta^W(\text{ESCS}_{ij}-\overline{\text{ESCS}}_{\cdot j}) + \beta^B\,\overline{\text{ESCS}}_{\cdot j} + \zeta_j + \varepsilon^{(m)}_{ij}. \] The two estimands are the within-school SES gradient \(\beta^W\) (how a student’s relative advantage predicts their score) and the between-school gradient \(\beta^B\) (how a school’s average advantage predicts its scores) — the latter the larger, more policy-salient quantity, and the one with more missing-information sensitivity (Section 4.1).

12.4 What we run, and on which weights

All three pipelines run on each country:

  • Pipeline A assembles the BRR-Fay target with the real assemble_pisa_brr_fay_target — \(M(R{+}1)=810\) survey-weighted lme4 fits per country — and Rubin-pools (the reference).
  • Pipeline C-Direct fits the stacked fractional bridge once in brms and calibrates with ccc_twolevel against that \(T_{\text{MI}}\).
  • Pipeline B reweights the same single fit per PV by PSIS and reports the Pareto \(\hat k\).

The student weight W_FSTUWT and the 80 replicate weights drive the design-based variance. A note on the weighting, because it matters for honesty: the substantive headline of the parent paper — that the USA–Korea between-school SES gap narrows and loses significance under the exact two-stage PISA weights (school weight times conditional student weight) — is a separate contribution, and we do not claim it here. This chapter’s question is narrower and methodological: do the three pipelines produce the same numbers on real data? That comparison — whether A, B, and C-Direct agree — holds for any fixed weighting, since all three share it; the estimates themselves of course depend on the weights, which is exactly why the parent paper’s exact-two-stage-weight story is a separate contribution. We use the standard final and replicate weights throughout and let that substantive weighting debate live in the paper proper.

Section 13.1 has the answer.
