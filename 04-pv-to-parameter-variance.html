4  From Plausible-Value Variance to Parameter Variance

4.1 The missing arrow

Your comment C3 sits right next to the term \((1+1/M)B\) in the Rubin total variance, and it asks the exactly right question: isn’t that \(B\) a variance of the plausible values \(y\), not of the model parameters \(\beta\)? How do the two connect? Your email broadened it to both pieces — “my best guess is that \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is coming from the plausible values \(y\) for the between and within.”

The short answer: no — both pieces live entirely in parameter space. The longer answer, which is this chapter, is that your instinct is half right and worth taking seriously. The variability in \(T_{\text{MI}}\) does originate in the \(M\) different plausible-value vectors \(y^{(1)},\dots,y^{(M)}\). But it is measured in the space of the regression coefficients \(\hat\beta_m \in \mathbb R^{p}\) (with \(p = \dim\beta_{\text{FE}} = 2K+1\), as in Section 3.1). The arrow from one space to the other is the GLS map, and drawing it explicitly is the whole job here.

4.2 The GLS map carries variance from \(y\)-space to \(\beta\)-space

From Section 3.1, the per-PV GLS estimator at a fixed precision \(\Omega = V^{-1}\) is \[ \hat\beta_m = (X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega\,y^{(m)} =: F\,y^{(m)}, \qquad m = 1,\dots,M, \] where \(F = (X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega\) is a fixed \(p\times n\) matrix (the same \(\Omega\) for every PV, under R3a). The single structural fact that matters is that \(F\) is linear. Linearity has an immediate covariance consequence: if we ask how the scatter across imputations propagates from \(y\)-space into \(\beta\)-space, the sandwich identity gives the exact answer, \[ \operatorname{Cov}_m\!\bigl[\hat\beta_m\bigr] = F\,\operatorname{Cov}_m\!\bigl[y^{(m)}\bigr]\,F^\top = (X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega \underbrace{\operatorname{Cov}_m\!\bigl[y^{(m)}\bigr]}_{n\times n,\ \text{in } y\text{-space}} \Omega X (X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}. \] This sandwich is the missing arrow. The plausible-value covariance — an \(n\times n\) object in observation space, the IRT measurement uncertainty — is pressed between \(F\) on the left and \(F^\top\) on the right and carried into a \(p\times p\) object in parameter space. From this point on, everything Rubin’s rules do is done in \(\mathbb R^{p\times p}\). (Here \(\operatorname{Cov}_m[\cdot]\) is the population imputation-posterior covariance; the \(B\) defined below is its finite-sample plug-in from \(M\) proper, exchangeable draws — which is exactly why the \(1/(M-1)\) and the \((1+1/M)\) correction appear.)

4.3 Both Rubin pieces are parameter-space matrices

With the map in hand, the two combining-rule pieces are unambiguous \(p\times p\) matrices.

Within-imputation variance \(\bar U\). For each PV, the BRR-Fay sandwich (PISA Technical Report Formula 10.9; \(a_d = 1/[R(1-k)^2] = 0.05\) for \(R=80,\,k=0.5\)) is \[ \hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}} = a_d\sum_{r=1}^R \bigl(\hat\beta_m^{(r)}-\hat\beta_m\bigr)\bigl(\hat\beta_m^{(r)}-\hat\beta_m\bigr)^\top, \qquad \hat\beta_m^{(r)} = (X^\top\Omega^{(r)}X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega^{(r)}y^{(m)}, \] already a \(p\times p\) object built from deviations of coefficient vectors. Its PV-average is \(\bar U = M^{-1}\sum_m \hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\) — the design-based sampling uncertainty in \(\hat\beta_m\) that would remain even if the latent scores were known perfectly.

Between-imputation variance \(B\). With \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}} = M^{-1}\sum_m\hat\beta_m\), \[ B = \frac{1}{M-1}\sum_{m=1}^M \bigl(\hat\beta_m-\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\bigr)\bigl(\hat\beta_m-\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\bigr)^\top. \] Look at what enters this formula: only the \(M\) coefficient vectors \(\hat\beta_1,\dots,\hat\beta_M\). The outcome vectors \(y^{(m)}\) do not appear. \(B\) is the sample covariance of \(M\) points in \(\mathbb R^{p}\) — it measures how far the GLS solution moves as the IRT model draws a fresh realization of the latent outcome. This is the direct answer to C3: \((1+1/M)B\) is not the variance of the PVs; it is the (corrected) covariance of the \(M\) GLS coefficient estimates, each the image of one PV vector under \(F\).

Total variance and the \((1+1/M)\) correction. \[ T_{\text{MI}} = \bar U + \Bigl(1+\tfrac1M\Bigr)B. \] The factor decomposes as \((1+1/M)B = B + B/M\): the first term is the between-imputation variance (already unbiased, thanks to the \(1/(M-1)\) in \(B\)); the extra \(B/M\) is the simulation error in the finite-\(M\) point estimate \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\), which averages only \(M\) imputed estimates around the unobservable infinite-\(M\) value (Rubin 1987). It is not a bias in \(B\). At \(M=10\) the inflation is \(1.1\) (and \(1.2\) at the \(M=5\) used below); as \(M\to\infty\) it vanishes and \(T_{\text{MI}}\to\bar U+B\). Every term is \(p\times p\); the whole apparatus operates in parameter space from start to finish.

4.4 “Between and within” — the symmetry your email asked about

Your email asked specifically whether the connection holds “for the between and within.” It does, and the symmetry is worth stating because it is the cleanest way to see that nothing is left in \(y\)-space. Both \(\bar U\) and \(B\) are \(\beta\)-space covariances produced by the same GLS machinery — they differ in what is perturbed, and in whether the perturbation lands on the map or on its input:

  • \(\bar U\) holds the PV draw fixed and perturbs the sampling design (the \(R=80\) replicate weights, which reweight \(\Omega \to \Omega^{(r)}\)); the resulting scatter of \(\hat\beta_m^{(r)}\) is a \(\beta\)-space covariance, then averaged over PVs. Here the perturbation falls on the map itself, \(F\to F^{(r)} = (X^\top\Omega^{(r)}X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega^{(r)}\).
  • \(B\) holds the sampling design fixed and perturbs the IRT draw (a new \(y^{(m)}\)); the resulting scatter of \(\hat\beta_m\) is a \(\beta\)-space covariance. Here the map \(F\) is held fixed and only its input \(y^{(m)}\) varies.

Two different sources of uncertainty — replicate weights for \(\bar U\), plausible-value draws for \(B\) — both measured in \(\hat\beta\)-space by the same GLS machinery. There is no residual sense in which any component of \(T_{\text{MI}}\) “is” a variance of \(y\).

4.5 Fraction of missing information

For a single coefficient, the fraction of missing information is \[ \gamma_k = \frac{(1+1/M)\,B_{kk}}{T_{\text{MI},kk}}, \] the share of total uncertainty in \(\hat\beta_k\) owed to not observing the latent scores directly. Illustrative magnitudes (typical of PISA secondary analyses, not measured here): within-school slopes \(\beta^W\) have low FMI, roughly \(0.03\)–\(0.08\), because student-level measurement noise averages down over thousands of students; between-school slopes \(\beta^B\) are higher, roughly \(0.10\)–\(0.20\), because the contextual effect is identified from only \(J\sim 150\)–\(500\) schools, so a fresh PV draw reshuffles school means more consequentially. The actual values are reported in the simulation diagnostics (Part IV) and the PISA runs (Part V).

4.6 Worked arithmetic (illustrative numbers throughout)

To make the propagation concrete, take a synthetic setting — \(J=10\) schools, \(n=200\) students, \(M=5\) PVs, \(\text{ICC}\approx0.30\) — and one coefficient, the within-school ESCS slope, in PISA-scale units\(^2\). These numbers are illustrative, drawn from a synthetic micro-example, not from a pipeline run.

\(m\) \(\hat\beta_m\) \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\)
1 40.12 2.30
2 39.88 2.18
3 40.05 2.27
4 40.31 2.21
5 39.64 2.34

From these, \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}} = 40.00\), \(\bar U = 2.260\), and \[ B = \tfrac14\sum_m(\hat\beta_m-40.00)^2 = \tfrac{0.2570}{4} = 0.0643, \qquad (1+\tfrac15)B = 1.2\times0.0643 = 0.0771, \qquad T_{\text{MI}} = 2.260+0.0771 = 2.3371. \] In standard-error terms, \(\sqrt{\bar U} = 1.503\) versus \(\sqrt{T_{\text{MI}}} = 1.529\) — the SE rises about \(1.7\%\). Small, but always in the same direction: ignoring \(B\) (using only \(\bar U\), or the stacked-bridge replicate variance \(V_{q,\text{rep}}\approx\bar U\) of Section 5.1) makes the interval too narrow and under-covers, because it pretends a different IRT draw would have left \(\hat\beta\) exactly where it is.

For a between-school coefficient \(\beta^B\) in the same synthetic setting, the school-level design inflates \(B\) more: illustratively \(\bar U^{(B)} = 18.0\), \(B^{(B)} = 2.25\), \((1+1/M)B^{(B)} = 2.70\), \(T_{\text{MI}}^{(B)} = 20.7\), so \(\sqrt{\bar U^{(B)}} = 4.243\) versus \(\sqrt{T_{\text{MI}}^{(B)}} = 4.550\) — about a \(7\%\) gap, i.e. FMI \(\approx 0.13\). A nominal 95% interval that drops \(B\) here covers about \(93\%\): coverage erodes more slowly than the SE near the center of a Gaussian, so a \(7\%\) SE shortfall costs roughly two coverage points, not seven — but the loss is real and one-directional.

4.7 Forward links

The between-imputation piece is now fully placed: \(B\) is the sample covariance of the \(M\) parameter vectors \(\hat\beta_m\), tied to the plausible values through the linear GLS map. The next chapter (Section 5.1) assembles the full \(T_{\text{MI}}\) from the \(R=80\) replicate weights — the \(M(R{+}1)\) fast lme4 REML refits that build each \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\), and the rubin_pool_matrix() routine that combines the pieces.

And the payoff for Pipeline C is now visible. Because both \(\bar U\) and \(B\) are honest covariance matrices in parameter space — not residuals, not score dispersions, not approximations — their sum \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is a well-defined, Cholesky-factorizable covariance target in the same space and units as the raw posterior covariance of the stacked MCMC draws. That dimensional match is exactly what lets the Cholesky Calibration Correction reshape the posterior to \(T_{\text{MI}}\) in one shot (Section 6.1). Had \(T_{\text{MI}}\) really been a variance of \(y\), the dimensions would not have matched and the calibration would be ill-defined. Tracing the GLS map from end to end is what guarantees they do.
