5  Where the External Variance Comes From: BRR-Fay Assembly

5.1 Your question, squarely answered

This is the chapter you asked for most directly. Two of your comments land on the same object:

C4 (on the external \(T_{\text{MI}}\)): I don’t understand where this comes from? isn’t it model specific and needs to be computed by estimating multiple models?

C2 (on \(T_{\text{MI}}\) generally): How do we get this without using A or B methods?

Both instincts are exactly right, and I want to say so plainly: \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is model-specific, and it does require fitting many models. The only correction is which fits. They are \(M(R{+}1) = 810\) fast frequentist lme4 REML fits — not MCMC chains — assembled by a self-contained subroutine, BRRFay-2L, that uses only lme4 and the published replicate weights and never calls any pipeline’s sampler. So “without A or B” does not mean the target is free or pipeline-specific; it means the variance machinery is orthogonal to the MCMC.

5.2 The per-PV BRR-Fay sandwich

PISA 2022 ships a final student weight W_FSTUWT and \(R = 80\) replicate weights W_FSTURWT1…W_FSTURWT80, built by Fay’s balanced repeated replication with shrinkage \(k = 0.5\). The PISA Technical Report (Formula 10.9) anchors the design variance with the multiplier \[ a_d = \frac{1}{R(1-k)^2} = \frac{1}{80\times 0.25} = 0.05. \] For each plausible value, let \(\hat\beta_m\) be the full-weight fixed-effect estimate and \(\hat\beta_m^{(r)}\) the estimate under replicate weight \(r\). Conceptually these are the GLS solves \(\hat\beta_m = (X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega y^{(m)}\) and \(\hat\beta_m^{(r)} = (X^\top\Omega^{(r)}X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega^{(r)}y^{(m)}\) (from Section 4.1); in the implementation each is obtained by a survey-weighted two-level REML fit, lme4::lmer(y ~ … + (1|school), weights = w_r), with the replicate weights scaled per Pfeffermann’s two-level convention. The per-PV BRR-Fay sandwich is the matrix form of the Fay variance, \[ \hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}} = a_d\sum_{r=1}^R \bigl(\hat\beta_m^{(r)}-\hat\beta_m\bigr)\bigl(\hat\beta_m^{(r)}-\hat\beta_m\bigr)^\top \in \mathbb R^{p\times p}, \] and the Rubin assembly of Section 4.1 gives \(\bar U = M^{-1}\sum_m\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\), the between term \(B\), and \[ T_{\text{MI}} = \bar U + \Bigl(1+\tfrac1M\Bigr)B. \] This is the whole of \(T_{\text{MI}}\): a \(p\times p\) object in parameter space, model-specific (it encodes the two-level model), assembled from many model fits — exactly as you suspected.

5.3 The 810-fit count, and why the fits are cheap

Per PV: one full-weight fit plus \(R = 80\) replicate fits \(= 81\). Over \(M = 10\) PVs, \[ M(R+1) = 10 \times 81 = 810 \text{ mini-fits per country}. \] That is the literal “multiple models” of your C4. The decisive fact is the cost of each. These are lme4 REML fits — iterative, but fast: in the deployed code (brr_fay_U_one_pv(), Section 8.1) each replicate is a full survey-weighted lmer(..., REML = TRUE) that re-estimates its own variance components, which is the standard (and conservative) BRR practice. I benchmarked one such fit at PISA-USA scale — synthetic \(n = 4{,}620\), \(J = 154\), two slopes — and it returns in about 0.012 s (code/01_brrfay_timing.R). At that rate the full 810-fit loop is on the order of ten seconds single-threaded; with a richer covariate set or default lmer controls, allow up to ~1–2 s per fit and the loop is still only 15–30 minutes. Either way it is orders of magnitude below MCMC.

The contrast is the point (the single-MCMC figures below are the times we measured on the real PISA data in Section 13.1; the Pipeline A total is ten times the measured single fit):

Pipeline A BRR-Fay loop Pipeline C MCMC
Fits per country \(M=10\) MCMC \(810\) lme4 REML \(1\) MCMC
Cost per fit $\(10–26 min | **\)$0.01–2 s (measured/bounded)** $\(10–26 min | | **Total per country** | **\)\(2–4 h** | **seconds to ~30 min** | **\)$10–26 min**

(The single-MCMC range is the measured USA-to-Korea span, ~10 min and ~26 min respectively, Section 13.1.) Had one tried to compute BRR-Fay variance by MCMC refits, the cost would be \(810\times 10\text{–}26\) min — weeks of wall-clock per country. That is why BRRFay-2L is committed to frequentist REML: not a corner cut, but the only feasible route, and a very cheap one. The PV loop parallelizes trivially over \(M\) cores, so even the pessimistic bound drops to a few minutes.

An optional speed-up, not the default. If the variance components are pinned at a single pilot \(\hat V\) (the R3a regime of Section 3.1), each replicate collapses to a genuine closed-form GLS solve — faster still, and exactly the setting in which Theorem 2.2 holds. The deployed code does not pin them; it re-fits fully per replicate. We flag the pinned variant only because it is the bridge between this chapter’s cost argument and Chapter 3’s exactness argument.

5.4 The BRRFay-2L algorithm

Deterministic throughout — an outer PV loop, an inner replicate loop, then a standard Rubin assembly. No sampler, no convergence audit beyond each REML fit’s own.

Algorithm BRRFay-2L
Inputs:  long-form data (M PVs, R=80 replicate weights, design X);
         a_d = 1/(R(1-k)^2) = 0.05
  1.  for m = 1..M:
        β̂_m ← weighted-REML fixef of y^(m) at full weight        # lme4::lmer
        for r = 1..R:
          β̂_m^(r) ← weighted-REML fixef of y^(m) at replicate weight W^(r)
        Û_m ← a_d · Σ_r (β̂_m^(r) − β̂_m)(β̂_m^(r) − β̂_m)ᵀ
  2.  β̄_Rubin ← M^{-1} Σ_m β̂_m
  3.  Ū ← M^{-1} Σ_m Û_m
  4.  B ← (M−1)^{-1} Σ_m (β̂_m − β̄_Rubin)(β̂_m − β̄_Rubin)ᵀ
  5.  T_MI ← Ū + (1 + 1/M) B
Output:  {β̂_m}, {Û_m}, β̄_Rubin, Ū, B, T_MI
        (optional R3a speed-up: pin variance components at a pilot V̂ → closed-form GLS solves)

5.5 BRRFay-2L is shared by all three pipelines

Here is the direct answer to C2’s “without A or B.” BRRFay-2L is not part of Pipeline A, B, or C-Direct — it is a pipeline-independent module that runs once and feeds all three:

Pipeline CCC center CCC target BRRFay-2L output used
A (orthodox) \(\hat\beta_m\) per PV \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\) per PV per-PV sandwiches (\(M\) calls)
B (PSIS) \(\hat\beta_m^{\text{PSIS}}\) per PV \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\) per PV per-PV sandwiches (\(M\) calls)
C-Direct \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) \(T_{\text{MI}}\) (pooled) pooled \(T_{\text{MI}}\) directly

Its inputs are only the replicate weights, the design, the PV vectors — its output is pure lme4 plus matrix algebra, independent of whether inference uses Stan, brms, or no sampler at all. No pipeline avoids the 810 fits; none pays them twice. Pipeline C’s economy over Pipeline A is not a saving on the variance target — both assemble the identical \(T_{\text{MI}}\) — but purely a saving on MCMC count, ten chains down to one. (One small asymmetry worth naming: Pipeline A uses its ten per-PV MCMC posterior means as CCC centers and the BRR-Fay sandwiches as targets. Pipeline C-Direct, by contrast, centers its single calibrated cloud at the external \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) — the BRR-Fay GLS pooled estimate itself, passed in as a numeric vector, not the raw stacked posterior mean — so its reported point equals Pipeline A’s exactly, and the R3a→R3b gap of Section 3.1 becomes a verification diagnostic that never enters the deliverable (Section 10.1, Section 13.1). The shared 810-fit cost is unaffected.) How each pipeline binds its target to the operator is Section 6.1.

5.6 Why the single MCMC’s own covariance cannot substitute

A tempting shortcut: surely the one stacked MCMC already carries the variance, making the external loop redundant? It does not, and the reason is structural rather than numerical. Define the stacked-bridge replicate variance \[ V_{q,\text{rep}} = a_d\sum_{r=1}^R \bigl(\hat\beta^{q,(r)}-\hat\beta^q\bigr)\bigl(\hat\beta^{q,(r)}-\hat\beta^q\bigr)^\top, \] where \(\hat\beta^{q,(r)}\) is the stacked-bridge point estimate under replicate \(r\). Under R3a and Theorem 2.2 applied per replicate (Section 3.1), \(\hat\beta^{q,(r)} = (X^\top\Omega^{(r)}X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega^{(r)}\bar y\) — a GLS solve on the PV-average \(\bar y\). The between-PV scatter is therefore collapsed into \(\bar y\) before any replicate perturbation, and is lost: \[ V_{q,\text{rep}} \;\overset{\text{R3a}}{=}\; \bar U \;\preceq\; T_{\text{MI}} = \bar U + (1+\tfrac1M)B. \] Read this in two registers. The Loewner inequality \(\preceq\) is the R3a statement: at a common plug-in \(V\), \(V_{q,\text{rep}}\) equals \(\bar U\) exactly and \((1+1/M)B \succeq 0\). The regime-free fact — the one that holds under R3a or R3b — is structural rather than Loewner: because \(\bar y\) collapses the between-PV scatter before any replicate perturbation, \(V_{q,\text{rep}}\) misses the entire \((1+1/M)B\) term outright. Under the deployed R3b sampler \(V_{q,\text{rep}} \approx \bar U\) is an empirical expectation (consistent with how Section 3.1 treats R3b), checked rather than assumed. So calibrating to \(V_{q,\text{rep}}\) rather than \(T_{\text{MI}}\) understates the SE by about \(\gamma/2\) — a first-order approximation, tight for the small within-school \(\gamma\) but running a little under the exact value for the larger between-school \(\gamma\) (the \(7\%\) quoted below is exact, against a \(\gamma/2\approx6.5\%\) linear estimate) — and under-covers, with \(\gamma = (1+1/M)B/T_{\text{MI}}\) the fraction of missing information. In the Section 4.1 micro-example, the within-school slope (\(\gamma\approx0.03\)) loses about \(1.7\%\) of SE — a nominal 95% interval still covers about 94% — while the between-school slope (\(\gamma\approx0.13\)) loses about \(7\%\), dropping coverage to roughly 93%. Modest per coefficient, but systematic, one-directional, and worse for the contextual (\(\beta^B\)) effects that are often the substantive target.

Two diagnostic ratios audit any implementation: \[ \rho_1 = \frac{V_{q,\text{rep}}}{\bar U}\quad(\text{expected}\approx 1,\ \textbf{measured not assumed}), \qquad \rho_2 = \frac{T_{\text{MI}}}{\bar U} = 1+\gamma \quad(\text{the FMI the single fit cannot see}). \] That \(\rho_2 > 1\) — and that it cannot be recovered from the stacked fit no matter how long the chain — is the numerical statement of why an external \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is genuinely necessary. As a guardrail, pipeline_c_direct.R refuses any Pipeline C call whose \(\Sigma_{\text{target}}\) is a self-target (equal to the raw posterior covariance) or an obvious mock matrix (Section 8.1) — a deliberate block against exactly the shortcut that would quietly under-cover.

5.7 The honest one-liner, and what comes next

\(T_{\text{MI}}\) is model-specific and takes 810 model fits — but cheap lme4 REML fits, a shared subroutine of seconds to (pessimistically) a few minutes per country, independent of any pipeline’s MCMC. Pipeline C-Direct’s sole advantage over Pipeline A is the MCMC count, one versus ten; the variance target costs the same either way, and neither pipeline can read it off a single posterior.

The next chapter (Section 6.1) shows how \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) and \(T_{\text{MI}}\) are injected into the Cholesky Calibration Correction as center and target, and why the operator acts on \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) alone. The implementation is assemble_pisa_brr_fay_target() with its helper brr_fay_U_one_pv() (Section 8.1).
