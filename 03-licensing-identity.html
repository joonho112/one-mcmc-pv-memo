3  The Licensing Identity: One Fit Gives the Pooled Point Estimate

3.1 Setup

This chapter does one thing: it proves that running one MCMC on the stacked fractional bridge and reading off the posterior mean over the fixed effects yields exactly the Rubin-pooled point estimate \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) — where, as I will be careful to define, \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) is the pooled estimate at a common plug-in variance. That algebraic fact — Theorem 2.2 — is the license for Pipeline C’s point estimate. It says nothing, by itself, about variance: the raw spread of the stacked posterior is not the right uncertainty, and correcting it is the job of Section 5.1 and Section 6.1. Here we nail only the center.

3.2 The two-level hybrid model

Students \(i\) are nested in schools \(j\). For plausible value \(m\), the Allison hybrid random-intercept model is \[ y_{ij}^{(m)} = \beta_1 + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \beta_k^{W}\bigl(x_{k,ij} - \bar x_{k\cdot j}\bigr) + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \beta_k^{B}\,\bar x_{k\cdot j} + \zeta_j + \epsilon_{ij}^{(m)}, \qquad \zeta_j \sim N(0,\sigma^2_{\text{school}}),\ \ \epsilon_{ij}^{(m)} \sim N(0,\sigma^2). \] The intercept \(\beta_1\), the within-school slopes \(\beta^W\), and the between-school slopes \(\beta^B\) are collected into the fixed-effect block \(\beta_{\text{FE}} = (\beta_1, \beta^W, \beta^B)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{p}\), \(p = 2K+1\) — the sole inferential target of this chapter. Marginalizing over \(\zeta_j\) gives a Gaussian with block-diagonal covariance \[ V = \operatorname{blkdiag}_j\!\bigl(\sigma^2 I_{n_j} + \sigma^2_{\text{school}}\,\mathbf 1_{n_j}\mathbf 1_{n_j}^\top\bigr), \qquad \Omega = V^{-1}. \] Writing \(X\) for the \(n\times p\) hybrid design (its columns are the constant, the within-school deviations \((x_{k,ij}-\bar x_{k\cdot j})\), and the school means \(\bar x_{k\cdot j}\)) and stacking students into \(y^{(m)}\in\mathbb R^n\), the per-PV GLS log-likelihood at fixed \(V\) is \[ \log L_m^0(\beta_{\text{FE}};V) = -\tfrac12\bigl(y^{(m)}-X\beta_{\text{FE}}\bigr)^\top\Omega\bigl(y^{(m)}-X\beta_{\text{FE}}\bigr) + \text{const}(V). \] The constant absorbs \(-\tfrac12\log\det V\), which does not depend on \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) at fixed \(V\) — the same \(\log\det V\) term that, because it is independent of \(y\), will make the variance-component score nonlinear later in this chapter.

A word on survey weights. PISA estimation is survey-weighted, and I have written \(\Omega\) without a weight matrix only for readability. Two facts make this harmless for the present identity. First, the survey weights index students and the sampling design — not which plausible value — so they are common across the \(M\) PVs and enter every \(L_m^0\) identically; that PV-invariance is the only thing the proof below uses. Second, our pipeline does not inject design-based uncertainty through the likelihood at all: \(L_m^0\) carries the model, and the design-based variance enters separately, through the external BRR-Fay target of Section 5.1. So whether one folds the (PV-common) weights into \(\Omega\) as a weighted pseudo-likelihood or keeps \(L_m^0\) as the unweighted working likelihood, the centering identity of this chapter is unaffected. The weights matter enormously for the variance — they are the heart of Section 5.1 — but not for the center.

3.3 The stacked fractional bridge

All three pipelines that use one MCMC sample from the stacked fractional bridge \[ \log q_{\text{SWL}}(\psi) = \log p(\psi) + \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\log L_m^0(\psi) + C, \qquad \psi = (\beta_{\text{FE}},\sigma^2_{\text{school}},\sigma^2). \] The \(1/M\) exponent is the crucial design choice, and it is worth seeing why in information terms. Each \(\log L_m^0\) carries Fisher information \(X^\top\Omega X\) for \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\). The naive stack \(\sum_m \log L_m^0\) would accumulate \(M\cdot X^\top\Omega X\) — fabricating \(M\)-fold replication, as if the ten plausible values were ten independent surveys rather than ten draws from one latent distribution. Multiplying each contribution by \(1/M\) restores exactly one dataset’s worth of information: \(q_{\text{SWL}} \propto p(\psi)\,[\prod_m L_m^0(\psi)]^{1/M}\) is the geometric mean of the \(M\) per-PV likelihoods. One MCMC fit, single-PV information content. In code the exponent is the per-row weight \(1/M\) on a long-stacked dataset (stack_fractional_posterior(); see Section 8.1).

3.4 Regularity conditions

The proof uses four conditions; I separate the one that controls exactness from the rest.

  • R0 — flat prior. \(p(\beta_{\text{FE}})\propto 1\). The identity is exact in the MLE/mode regime. A proper Gaussian prior does not break the stacked-versus-per-PV correspondence — under a common prior the stacked-bridge MAP still coincides with the per-PV MAP (verified in falsify_theorem22.R §F3). What a proper prior adds is shrinkage relative to the flat-prior Rubin target; where a weakly informative prior is used for sampler stability we keep it diffuse enough that this shrinkage gap is negligible.
  • R2 — common design. \(X\) (and any PV-common survey weighting) is identical across the \(M\) PVs. Automatic for PISA: plausible values vary the outcome, not the covariates — provided the cluster means \(\bar x_{k\cdot j}\) are computed once from the full sample, not per imputation.
  • R3a — common plug-in \(V\). A single pilot \((\hat\sigma^2,\hat\sigma^2_{\text{school}})\) is fixed, so \(\Omega\) is the same matrix in every per-PV solve and in the stacked solve. This is the condition that makes the identity exact; its relation to the real (joint) MCMC and to conventional separate-fit pooling is the subject of the honesty section below.
  • R5 — invertibility. \(X^\top\Omega X\) is positive definite — equivalently, \(X\) has full column rank and \(\Omega\succ 0\) — which holds for the PISA USA and KOR designs.

3.5 Theorem 2.2 (the licensing identity)

Let \(\hat\beta_{\text{FE},m} = (X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega\,y^{(m)}\) be the per-PV GLS estimate at the common plug-in \(\Omega\) of R3a, and let \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}} = M^{-1}\sum_m \hat\beta_{\text{FE},m}\) be the corresponding (fixed-\(\Omega\)) Rubin pooled estimate. Let \(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}^q\) be the maximizer of \(\log q_{\text{SWL}}\) over \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) at that same fixed \(V\).

Theorem 2.2. Under R0, R2, R3a, R5, \[ \hat\beta_{\text{FE}}^q = \arg\max_{\beta_{\text{FE}}}\frac1M\sum_{m=1}^M\log L_m^0(\beta_{\text{FE}};V) = \frac1M\sum_{m=1}^M \hat\beta_{\text{FE},m} = \bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}. \] Moreover, under R0 the \(q\)-marginal in \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) is Gaussian, so its mode and mean coincide: \(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}^q = \mathbb E_q[\beta_{\text{FE}}]\). The equality is exact in arithmetic; in finite precision the residual sits at the floating-point level (verified numerically in Part IV).

Two clarifications belong with the statement. First, the “mode = mean” clause is what lets Pipeline C read its point estimate straight off the stacked posterior mean — no separate per-PV refit is needed to obtain the center. Second, and this is essential to state honestly: \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) here is the fixed-\(\Omega\) pooled estimate. It equals the number a practitioner would get from \(M\) separately fitted mixed models — each of which re-estimates its own \(\Omega_m = V_m^{-1}\) — only up to the R3a-vs-R3b gap of the next section. The separate-fit pool is an R3b object; Theorem 2.2 is an R3a statement.

3.5.1 Proof

Step 1 — per-PV score. Differentiating \(\log L_m^0\) in \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\), \[ \nabla_{\beta_{\text{FE}}}\log L_m^0 = X^\top\Omega\,y^{(m)} - X^\top\Omega X\,\beta_{\text{FE}}. \] This score is affine-linear in \(y^{(m)}\), and the term \(X^\top\Omega X\,\beta_{\text{FE}}\) does not depend on \(m\). That linearity is the load-bearing fact of the whole theorem.

Step 2 — stacked gradient. Under R0 the prior gradient vanishes, so \[ \nabla_{\beta_{\text{FE}}}\Bigl[\tfrac1M\textstyle\sum_m\log L_m^0\Bigr] = \frac1M\sum_{m=1}^M\bigl[X^\top\Omega\,y^{(m)} - X^\top\Omega X\,\beta_{\text{FE}}\bigr] = X^\top\Omega\,\bar y - X^\top\Omega X\,\beta_{\text{FE}}, \] with \(\bar y = M^{-1}\sum_m y^{(m)}\). R2 (common \(X\), common weighting) and R3a (common \(\Omega\)) are exactly what let \(X^\top\Omega X\) and \(X^\top\Omega\) come out of the sum; if either varied with \(m\), the average could not collapse to a single matrix acting on \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\).

Step 3 — stacked normal equation. Setting the gradient to zero and using R5, \[ X^\top\Omega X\,\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}^q = X^\top\Omega\,\bar y \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \hat\beta_{\text{FE}}^q = (X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega\,\bar y. \]

Step 4 — average the per-PV equations. Each per-PV normal equation is \(X^\top\Omega X\,\hat\beta_{\text{FE},m} = X^\top\Omega\,y^{(m)}\) (same \(\Omega\), by R3a); averaging over \(m\), \[ X^\top\Omega X\,\Bigl[\tfrac1M\textstyle\sum_m\hat\beta_{\text{FE},m}\Bigr] = X^\top\Omega\,\bar y. \] The stacked equation (Step 3) and the averaged per-PV equations have identical right-hand sides; by R5 the solution is unique, so \(\hat\beta_{\text{FE}}^q = M^{-1}\sum_m\hat\beta_{\text{FE},m} = \bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\).

Step 5 — mode equals mean. At fixed \(V\) (R3a) with a flat prior (R0), \(\log q_{\text{SWL}}\) is quadratic in \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) with Hessian \(-X^\top\Omega X \prec 0\) (R5), so the block is exactly Gaussian and its mode equals its mean. Note that under R3a the variance components are fixed, not sampled, so for \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) the marginal and conditional posteriors coincide — the Gaussianity is genuine, not an artifact of integrating out \(\sigma^2\) (which would generally leave heavier, \(t\)-like tails, exactly the phenomenon behind the variance-component discussion below). \(\qquad\square\)

In one sentence: “average the gradients” equals “evaluate at \(\bar y\),” so the stacked bridge solves the same normal equations as GLS on the PV-averaged outcome.

3.6 R3a vs R3b: exactness and its honest limit

This is the place I most want to be careful with you, because glossing it would undermine the whole pipeline. The proof lives in R3a world: one pilot \(\hat V\) frozen and reused in every solve. There the identity is exact, and our Part IV checks put the residual at the floating-point level.

The deployed Pipeline C MCMC lives in R3b world: it samples \((\beta_{\text{FE}},\sigma^2_{\text{school}},\sigma^2)\) jointly, so \(\Omega\) is not pinned but co-evolves with the chain, and the marginal \(p(\beta_{\text{FE}}\mid\text{data})\) is a mixture over \(V\). Because \(\partial\hat\beta(V)/\partial V\neq 0\), the R3a algebra does not transfer at theorem level to the R3b mode. The same point explains B1’s subtlety: the conventional practice — ten separately fitted mixed models, each with its own \(\hat V_m\), then pooled — is likewise an R3b comparison, and differs from the fixed-\(\Omega\) \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) of Theorem 2.2 by a term of the same kind.

The point is not that this gap is harmless but that we measure it rather than assume it away. For each coefficient we report the standardized discrepancy \[ \Delta_{\text{R3b},k} = \frac{\bigl|\hat\beta_k^{\text{R3b}} - \hat\beta_k^{\text{R3a}}\bigr|}{\sqrt{T_{\text{MI},kk}}}, \] where \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is the total MI variance defined in Section 5.1 — borrowed here only as a scale, so the ratio asks “is the R3a-vs-R3b shift small relative to the uncertainty we ultimately report?” We apply a traffic-light rule — green \(<0.01\), yellow \(\le 0.02\), red \(>0.02\). There is reason to expect green at PISA scale: the variance-component posterior concentrates tightly (the posterior SD of \(\sigma^2_{\text{school}}\) is a small fraction of its mean), so \(\hat V\) is a good stand-in for the posterior-mean \(V\). But that is an empirical expectation, checked in the known-truth simulation of Part IV and on the real data in Part V — not a theorem. If a coefficient lands red, we report the R3b result directly rather than lean on the identity.

3.7 Why the identity does not extend to the variance components

Theorem 2.2 exploits one structure only: the FE score is linear in \(y^{(m)}\). The score for \(\sigma^2_{\text{school}}\) is not. Differentiating the marginal log-likelihood gives \[ \frac{\partial \log L_m^0}{\partial \sigma^2_{\text{school}}} = -\tfrac12\operatorname{tr}\!\Bigl(\Omega\,\tfrac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma^2_{\text{school}}}\Bigr) + \tfrac12\,r_m^\top\,\Omega\,\tfrac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma^2_{\text{school}}}\,\Omega\,r_m, \qquad r_m = y^{(m)}-X\beta_{\text{FE}}. \] The first term does not involve \(y^{(m)}\) at all (it is the derivative of the \(\log\det V\) we set aside earlier, and it cannot be averaged away); the second is quadratic in \(y^{(m)}\), and \(M^{-1}\sum_m y^{(m)}(y^{(m)})^\top \neq \bar y\,\bar y^\top\) unless all PVs are identical. So “average the \(m\) scores” \(\neq\) “evaluate at \(\bar y\),” and the stacked mode for \(\sigma^2_{\text{school}}\) does not equal the Rubin average of the per-PV variance-component estimates. We expect this drift to be small at PISA scale, but the chapter does not rely on any particular figure — we report the measured value in Part IV. What matters here is the structural fact that the drift is nonzero, unlike the \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) case. This is the formal reason the calibration in Section 6.1 touches the \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) block only, and why calibrating the variance components is deferred to the follow-up paper.

3.8 Pipeline B does not inherit Theorem 2.2

It is worth saying explicitly that Pipeline B’s point estimates do not enjoy the exact identity. Pipeline B forms per-PV estimates as Pareto-smoothed importance-weighted averages of the shared bridge draws, \(\hat\beta_{\text{FE},m}^{\text{PSIS}} = \sum_s \tilde w_{m,s}\,\beta_s\) (Section 7.1). The algebraic cancellation in the proof requires the exact maximizer of the gradient equation; a finite-sample weighted average is not that maximizer. PSIS recovers the per-PV target only as the number of draws \(S\to\infty\); in the finite-variance regime (\(\hat k_m < 0.5\)) the residual shrinks at the \(O_p(S^{-1/2})\) rate, and more slowly as the Pareto shape \(\hat k_m\) rises toward the unreliable regime \(\hat k_m \ge 0.7\), at which point Pipeline B falls back to A or C-Direct (Vehtari et al. 2024). Theorem 2.2’s exactness is a Pipeline C-Direct statement, full stop.

3.9 Lineage and verification

In the spirit of Si and Reiter (2011), who compared posterior simulation against the combining rules for multiple imputation, the single-level version of this identity is classical; Theorem 2.2 extends it to the two-level Allison-hybrid GLS setting, to fractional PV stacking, and adds the explicit R3a/R3b decomposition that does not arise in the single-level case. We do not ask you to take the identity on faith: the known-truth simulation in Part IV reports an exactness grid — coefficients across a factorial of \(J\), cluster size, ICC, and \(M\) — and we expect every cell to pass to floating-point precision under R3a, shown beside the contrasting \(\Delta_{\text{R3b}}\) sensitivities.

What this chapter licensed: the center. One stacked fit returns the fixed-\(\Omega\) \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) as its posterior mean — exactly under R3a, with a measured gap under R3b and relative to conventional separate-fit pooling. The variance story — what \(T_{\text{MI}} = \bar U + (1+1/M)B\) is, and why the between-imputation term lives in parameter space — is where we turn next.
