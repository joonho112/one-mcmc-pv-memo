Preface

Matt — thank you for the careful read of the proposal and for the five comments you left in the margin. They land on exactly the right places. This short book is my answer. Its only goal is to make Pipeline B and especially Pipeline C legible: to show, from four independent angles, that they do what the proposal claims, and in particular to answer your central question — where does the external total variance \(T_{\mathrm{MI}}\) come from, and does it secretly require fitting the model many times?

The one-sentence answer, which the rest of the book unpacks, is:

The short version. \(T_{\mathrm{MI}}\) is model-specific and it does require many fits — but those are cheap, fast lme4 REML replicate refits (\(M(R{+}1)\) of them), not MCMC runs. That cost is shared by all three pipelines; Pipeline C’s saving lives entirely in the number of MCMC runs (ten → one). Nothing about the variance target is unique to Pipeline C, and nothing about it is free — it is the same BRR-Fay machinery the field already runs.

How this book is organized

Each chapter is short and aimed at one question. The book answers from four angles in turn:

  1. Mathematics — the identity that licenses the point estimate, and the exact origin of the variance target (Part II).
  2. Code — the actual functions that implement each operator, mapped one-to-one to the math (Part III).
  3. A controlled simulation — synthetic data with a known ground truth, where we watch Pipeline C recover the truth and reproduce Pipeline A (Part IV).
  4. PISA 2022 — the three pipelines run side by side on the real data, USA and Korea (Part V).

A note on scope

This is a demonstration, not the manuscript. The simulations are deliberately at a smoke / pilot scale — small enough to run quickly and transparently, large enough to make the point. The aim is clarity and correctness, not the final production numbers. Where a claim is exact, we prove it; where it is approximate, we say so and show the diagnostic that flags it.

— JoonHo
