7  Pipeline B: Importance Sampling, Tempering, and Diagnostics

7.1 A collaborative starting point

This chapter takes its brief from your comment beside the bridge formula:

C1: This seems like a good starting point, esp for the point estimates. For importance sampling we may want to further fit a second time and further temper to get better proposal values that are more likely to include the modes from the replicate distributions. Should double check the pareto version of importance sampling.

and from your email:

…higher multiples of \(M\) (say \(1/2M\) or \(1/3M\)) to flatten out the proposal distribution … Each of the \(M\) target distributions could be shifted away from the overall mode … the mode could be islands with less overlap … too much tempering could skew the point estimates, hence wanting to run it for the \(1/M\) first.

These are not points to rebut — they are the design brief for Pipeline B as it stands. The Pareto check you asked for is built in; the tempering you sketched is a single code knob; the second-fit idea is a real option. The rest of the chapter explains why all three cohere.

7.2 The Pipeline B algorithm

Pipeline B shares one stacked MCMC fit with Pipeline C-Direct, then treats the bridge as a proposal and each per-PV posterior as a target, recovering per-PV summaries by importance reweighting.

  1. One stacked MCMC. Fit \(q_{\text{SWL}}(\psi)\propto p(\psi)\prod_m L_m^0(\psi)^{1/M}\) once, giving draws \(\{\beta_s\}_{s=1}^S\) of \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\).
  2. Per-PV importance weights. For each PV, \[ \log w_{m,s} = \log L_m^0(\beta_s) - \frac1M\sum_{m'=1}^M\log L_{m'}^0(\beta_s), \] the per-PV log-likelihood minus the log geometric-mean bridge density.
  3. PSIS smoothing. Pass log_r = log_p − log_q to loo::psis(); a generalized Pareto fit to the upper tail returns smoothed weights \(\tilde w_{m,s}\) and the shape diagnostic \(\hat k_m\) (Vehtari et al. 2024).
  4. Per-PV center. \(\hat\beta_m^{\text{PSIS}} = \sum_s \tilde w_{m,s}\,\beta_s\) — an \(O_p(S^{-1/2})\) estimator, not the exact identity of Section 3.1 (that is why Pipeline B does not inherit Theorem 2.2).
  5. Weighted CCC. Calibrate each per-PV cloud to the survey-design target \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\) with center \(\hat\beta_m^{\text{PSIS}}\) and the PSIS weights, using the self-normalized weighted covariance of the draws before the Cholesky map (Section 6.1).
  6. Rubin pool. \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}} = M^{-1}\sum_m\hat\beta_m^{\text{PSIS}}\), \(T_{\text{MI}} = \bar U + (1+1/M)B\); all SEs and intervals come from \((\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}},T_{\text{MI}})\).

The runner is pipeline_b_stacked_psis(); the PSIS core is pipeline_b_psis_density_pair() (Section 8.1).

7.3 The Pareto \(\hat k\) gate: “double check the pareto version,” formalized

Your instruction to “double check the pareto version” is exactly the \(\hat k_m\) diagnostic. \(\hat k_m\) measures how heavy the upper tail of the weight distribution is — how much of the total weight a few draws carry. The standard tiers (Vehtari et al. 2024) (sample-size-dependent rules of thumb, not sharp cutoffs):

\(\hat k_m\) Status Action
\(<0.5\) Green — reliable report Pipeline B
\([0.5,0.7)\) Yellow — caution report B with per-PV \(\hat k\) + mandatory C-Direct cross-check
\(\ge 0.7\) Red — unreliable fall back to Pipeline A for that PV, or to C-Direct entirely

In code, khat <- max(loo::pareto_k_values(psis_obj)) per PV. The gate is the safety valve: where \(\hat k<0.7\), B and C-Direct agree in point and B adds per-PV richness; where \(\hat k\ge0.7\), C-Direct is valid because it never builds importance weights. One caveat the gate does not cover: a small \(\hat k\) certifies the importance sampling is stable, not that B’s pooled standard errors match the orthodox target — on the real PISA data (Section 13.1) B’s intervals run narrow even at \(\hat k\le0.07\), because B reweights one shared draw cloud. So “report Pipeline B” means its point and its \(\hat k\); its intervals stay a cross-check against C-Direct. Whether PISA \(\hat k\) stays \(<0.7\) is an empirical question we test in Section 11.1 and confirm in Section 13.1, not assert.

7.4 Island modes: the geometry you anticipated

Why can the bridge be a poor IS proposal? Because \(q_{\text{SWL}}\propto\prod_m(L_m^0)^{1/M}\) is a geometric mean: it places mass where all \(M\) per-PV likelihoods are simultaneously large. When the per-PV posteriors overlap (small \(n\), weak identification), the bridge sits near every mode and the ratio \(L_m^0/q_{\text{SWL}}\) has a bounded tail. But the modes need not coincide — each \(y^{(m)}\) is a distinct IRT draw, so the \(M\) modes scatter in \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) space. As \(n\) grows, each per-PV posterior sharpens and the modes separate into an archipelago; the geometric-mean bridge settles in the valley between islands, draws land in low-likelihood regions for each individual PV, and the few that do reach PV \(m\)’s mode get inflated weight — driving \(\hat k_m\) up. The unwelcome irony you spotted: the large-\(n\), well-identified regime where you most want Pipeline B is exactly where the \(1/M\) proposal is most strained. High-ICC settings (Korea’s mathematics ICC \(\approx 0.45\)) may widen the cross-PV spread more than low-ICC ones (USA \(\approx 0.20\); the reading data analyzed here is milder, near \(0.2\) for both countries), so this is worth watching empirically.

7.5 Your tempering idea, formalized

The fix you sketched is to replace the exponent \(1/M\) with \(1/(kM)\), \(k>1\): \[ q^{(k)}(\psi)\propto p(\psi)\prod_{m=1}^M L_m^0(\psi)^{1/(kM)}. \] A larger \(k\) raises the temperature: the proposal broadens and covers separated modes better, lowering \(\hat k\). The implementation is a small change to the fractional-weight rule inside stack_fractional_posterior(), where the per-row weight is currently hard-coded to 1/M (w_frac <- 1/M); setting it to 1/(k*M) gives your \(1/2M, 1/3M\). It is not yet a user-facing argument, and because the fractional weight is woven into the provenance hash, the tempered bridge is run as a deliberate sensitivity branch, not a casual toggle (Section 8.1).

You also flagged the risk — “too much tempering could skew the point estimates” — and the answer has two parts. Under the flat-prior regime of Theorem 2.2 (assumption R0), the mode is exponent-invariant: the proof (Section 3.1) uses only that the GLS gradient is linear in \(y^{(m)}\), and a scalar \(1/(kM)\) in front of the log-likelihood leaves the normal equation unchanged, so the stacked mode of \(q^{(k)}\) is \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) for every \(k\). With a proper prior — the brms default — this is no longer exact: tempering rescales the likelihood relative to the fixed prior precision, so the MAP drifts toward the prior mean as \(k\) grows. The drift is small when the prior is diffuse, but not zero, and the codebase’s own exactness harness is built to measure it. So your worry is real but bounded, with two mitigations: keep the prior weak, and — your own instinct — run \(1/M\) first. Even setting the prior aside, a flatter bridge gives more dispersed draws, so the PSIS-reweighted mean \(\hat\beta_m^{\text{PSIS}}\) (a draw-average, not the mode) carries more Monte-Carlo noise — at the \(O_p(S^{-1/2})\) rate when \(\hat k_m<0.5\), and more slowly as \(\hat k_m\) rises toward the gate. \(1/M\) is the most concentrated proposal consistent with the mode guarantee; \(\hat k\) tells you at once whether a flatter one is needed. The \(k\in\{2,3\}\) range is exploratory; we report the \(\hat k\)-versus-dispersion tradeoff on known-truth data in Section 11.1.

7.6 Your two-MCMC option

Your “further fit a second time” is a real, targeted strategy. Run the \(1/M\) fit as usual and take point estimates from it; if some \(\hat k_m\) fail the gate, run a second, more diffuse fit at \(1/(kM)\) purely as the IS proposal, and recompute those PVs’ weights against it. The cost is one extra MCMC (≈10–26 min per country at PISA scale, Section 13.1), spent only where the gate flags trouble — a rescue that avoids falling all the way back to ten per-PV Pipeline A fits. The first run preserves efficient estimates; the second services the tails. It is implemented through the same w_frac knob, with no change to the PSIS or CCC logic downstream, and is evaluated in Section 11.1. Like the tempering levels, it is exploratory, not yet a validated default.

7.7 Pipeline B and C-Direct: complementary, not rivals

C-Direct is the robust default for the PISA reporting: one deterministic CCC pass anchored by Theorem 2.2, no PSIS, no \(\hat k\) risk. Pipeline B adds a diagnostic layer C-Direct cannot: per-PV \(\hat\beta_m^{\text{PSIS}}\) and per-PV \(\hat k_m\) give a direct view of whether the single stacked posterior is spreading its mass correctly across the \(M\) per-PV posteriors. All \(\hat k_m<0.5\) is confirmatory that the bridge’s tails are well-behaved and that B’s point covers the modes. It does not, by itself, certify B’s variance: because B’s \(M\) per-PV estimates reweight one shared draw cloud, the pooled between-imputation term \(B\) can run below the independent-refit target even when every \(\hat k_m\) is deep green — as it does on the real PISA data (Section 13.1). A drift into Yellow sharpens the warning, but the gap is structural, not gated by it. The workflow follows: run C-Direct as default, run B alongside to verify \(\hat k\) and the per-PV point estimates, and treat B’s intervals as a cross-check, not the deliverable. Where B’s gate fails, C-Direct stands alone.

7.8 Honest scope and attribution

Three commitments. First, tempering at \(1/2M\), \(1/3M\) and the two-MCMC variant are exploratory — your ideas, implemented as sensitivity knobs, not yet validated at PISA scale; Section 11.1 reports the evidence. Second, “PISA \(\hat k<0.7\)” is an expectation we test, not a finding asserted here. Third, on credit: the PSIS step is shared with Jedhoff et al. (2025) (who paired it with IWMM in a generic two-step setting); what is distinct in Pipeline B is the coupling of PSIS with the Williams–Savitsky CCC calibration against an external BRR-Fay target, the two-level PV likelihood ratio, and the survey-design integration — the CCC calibration, not IWMM, is the load-bearing divergence.
