6  The Cholesky Calibration Correction, and Why Only the Betas

6.1 Two questions in one comment

C5: fixing the variance components? why not do importance resampling for the joint distribution instead of the conditional/profile version?

Your fifth comment bundles two distinct challenges. The first is about scope: why does the calibration touch only the fixed-effect block \(\beta_{\text{FE}} = (\beta_1,\beta^W,\beta^B)^\top\in\mathbb R^p\) and leave the variance components \((\sigma^2_{\text{school}},\sigma^2)\) exactly as the sampler produced them? The second is about method: granted we are calibrating something, why a deterministic Cholesky reshape rather than importance-resampling the joint posterior \(\psi\)? I answer both, in order.

6.2 The CCC operator

The Cholesky Calibration Correction (CCC) is a deterministic, one-shot affine map on a draw cloud. For draws \(\{\beta_s\}_{s=1}^S\subset\mathbb R^p\), a target center \(c\in\mathbb R^p\), and an SPD target covariance \(\Sigma_{\text{target}}\in\mathbb R^{p\times p}\), \[ \beta_s^{\text{cal}} = c + A\,(\beta_s - \bar\beta^{\text{raw}}), \qquad A = L_{\text{target}}\,L_{\text{raw}}^{-1}, \] where \(\bar\beta^{\text{raw}} = S^{-1}\sum_s\beta_s\) and \(L_{\text{raw}}, L_{\text{target}}\) are the lower-triangular Cholesky factors of the raw empirical covariance \(\Sigma_{\text{raw}}\) and of \(\Sigma_{\text{target}}\) (\(L L^\top = \Sigma\)).

The R trap. Base R chol(Σ) returns the upper-triangular factor \(U\) with \(U^\top U = \Sigma\); the lower factor required here is t(chol(Σ)). Getting this wrong silently transposes \(A\) and miscalibrates the cloud in a way that survives a casual eye-check but fails the matrix residual diagnostic below.

Deterministic, unlike IWMM. Importance-weighted moment matching (IWMM) corrects moments by iteratively reweighting draws until the weighted mean and covariance converge, accumulating stochastic noise along the way. CCC closes in a single pass — factor \(\Sigma_{\text{raw}}\) and \(\Sigma_{\text{target}}\) once, apply the affine map, done. No iteration, no convergence criterion, no random restart. That determinism is a genuine practical advantage when the same operator must run inside every pipeline.

6.3 Three invariants

The map satisfies three invariants by construction.

  1. Mean. \(\operatorname{mean}\{\beta_s^{\text{cal}}\} = c\): centering at \(-\bar\beta^{\text{raw}}\) then shifting by \(+c\) places the centroid exactly at \(c\).
  2. Covariance. \(\operatorname{Cov}\{\beta_s^{\text{cal}}\} = \Sigma_{\text{target}}\) to machine precision, since \(A\Sigma_{\text{raw}}A^\top = L_{\text{target}}L_{\text{raw}}^{-1}\Sigma_{\text{raw}}(L_{\text{raw}}^{-1})^\top L_{\text{target}}^\top = L_{\text{target}}L_{\text{target}}^\top = \Sigma_{\text{target}}\). A hand implementation in this project’s de-risk verified the residual \(\|A\Sigma_{\text{raw}}A^\top - \Sigma_{\text{target}}\|_{\max} \approx 4\times 10^{-17}\) in double precision. Two qualifications: under PSIS weights (Pipeline B) it is the weighted covariance that equals \(\Sigma_{\text{target}}\) exactly, not the equal-weight one; and if the nearest-PD repair fires, the identity holds against the repaired \(\Sigma_{\text{raw}}\).
  3. Block. The map acts on \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) only; the variance-component draws are appended unchanged (in code, .ccc_block_expand() places \(A\) in the FE block and the identity in the VC block).

A precision caveat, stated honestly. Invariants 1–2 match the first two moments exactly. CCC does not claim to correct the posterior beyond mean and covariance: the affine map carries higher-order structure of the raw cloud through linearly — skewness and kurtosis are linearly rescaled, not removed. CCC is therefore an exact moment-matching operation, and an exact distributional correction only when the raw draws are Gaussian. For the linear-Gaussian \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) block under a flat prior that is the relevant case (Section 3.1), but the limitation is worth naming.

The MCMC boundary, stated honestly. In exactly that linear-Gaussian case, the C-Direct deliverable is itself closed-form: \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) is a GLS solve and \(T_{\text{MI}}\) a BRR-Fay assembly, and the CCC reshapes any input cloud to those two moments. One could report \((\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}, T_{\text{MI}})\) with no sampler at all — as could the orthodox path, since here the two coincide by Theorem 2.2. Far from a weakness, that is why this case is the right place to verify the machinery: an exact answer exists to check against (Section 10.1). The stacked MCMC earns its keep where the framework is actually aimed: nonlinear links (logistic or ordinal item-response models), genuinely joint variance-component inference, and non-Gaussian posteriors — settings with no closed-form \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) or \(T_{\text{MI}}\), where the one-fit-plus-CCC route is the only practical one and the orthodox alternative is not a formula but ten MCMC fits. The “one MCMC, not ten” economy is what that general case buys; the linear-Gaussian demonstration is what proves the single fit lands in the right place.

6.4 Per-pipeline binding

The operator is pipeline-independent; only its three arguments change.

Pipeline CCC calls center \(c\) target \(\Sigma_{\text{target}}\)
A (orthodox) \(M\) (per PV) \(\hat\beta_m\) \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\)
B (stacked-PSIS) \(M\) (PSIS-weighted) \(\hat\beta_m^{\text{PSIS}} = \sum_s\tilde w_{m,s}\beta_s\) \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\)
C-Direct \(1\) (pooled) \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) \(T_{\text{MI}} = \bar U + (1+1/M)B\)

The \(T_{\text{MI}}\) caution. \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is a pre-pooling CCC target only for C-Direct, which calibrates a single pooled cloud. For A and B the per-PV target is \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\); injecting \(T_{\text{MI}}\) per PV would over-disperse each cloud by \((1+1/M)B\), since the between-imputation term \(B\) is a property of the Rubin pooling step, not of any single PV’s posterior. In A and B, \(T_{\text{MI}}\) emerges post-pooling.

The center is external, by construction. In the deployed code C-Direct’s center \(c\) is the numeric \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) — the BRR-Fay GLS pooled estimate, passed in as an external vector, not the raw stacked posterior mean. So the center column above is literal: because the map places the calibrated centroid at \(c\) regardless of where the raw cloud sits (invariant 1), C-Direct’s reported point equals Pipeline A’s exactly, and the R3a→R3b distance between the stacked posterior mean and \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) becomes a verification diagnostic, never carried into the deliverable (Section 10.1, Section 13.1).

6.5 Why only \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\): three compounding reasons

1 — Theorem 2.2 supplies an exact target only for \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\). The identity of Section 3.1 rests on the GLS gradient being linear in \(y^{(m)}\), which makes the stacked-bridge mode for \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) exactly \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) — a closed-form calibration center. The profile score for \(\sigma^2_{\text{school}}\) carries a \(-\tfrac12\log\det V\) term and a quadratic in \(y^{(m)}\), both nonlinear; “average the per-PV scores” is no longer “evaluate at \(\bar y\),” and no clean Rubin-pool anchor results. The stacked mode for \(\sigma^2_{\text{school}}\) drifts from the Rubin-averaged value by a small amount — on the order of a percent or two at PISA scale, which we report in Part IV rather than assume (consistent with the R3a/R3b honesty of Section 3.1). A calibration whose own anchor is approximate cannot ground a precise CCC step.

2 — No design-based variance-component target is built in this paper. The CCC targets for \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) — \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\) and \(T_{\text{MI}}\) — come from the BRR-Fay loop of Section 5.1, which is design-valid because \(\hat\beta_m\) is (conceptually) linear in the weights. A parallel BRR-Fay loop on variance components — forming a replicate sandwich of \(\hat\sigma^2_{\text{school},m}\) — would need its own replicate-REML routine and raises identification questions under PV stacking. That is the scope of the follow-up paper (the Williams–McGuire–Savitsky variance-component framework), not this one. Without a design-based VC target, there is nothing for a VC-CCC step to match.

3 — Support preservation: the structural safety reason. This is the strongest of the three and it is structural, not epistemic. \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\in\mathbb R^p\) is unconstrained, so the affine map is valid everywhere — it cannot move a draw out of the parameter space. Variance components obey \(\sigma^2,\sigma^2_{\text{school}}>0\); their support is the positive orthant, and an affine map applied to draws near the boundary can push them negative, violating the constraint — no choice of target rescues an affine reshape on the natural scale. (A variance-component calibration would instead work on an unconstrained reparameterization such as \(\log\sigma^2\) and exponentiate back — exactly the kind of extra machinery we defer to the follow-up paper.) On the natural scale, the CCC operator and variance-component geometry are simply incompatible.

6.6 Why not jointly importance-resample the whole vector?

Your alternative — importance-resample \(\psi = (\beta_{\text{FE}},\sigma^2_{\text{school}},\sigma^2)\) jointly — is coherent, and it is worth being precise about what it would mean.

First, CCC is not resampling. Every draw the sampler produced appears in the calibrated output, with no thinning and no duplication; it is a reshape, not a redraw. Importance resampling instead draws a new sample with probabilities proportional to weights, introducing discrete approximation error and discarding low-weight draws.

Second, joint importance resampling of \(\psi\) over the \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) block is exactly what Pipeline B does (Section 7.1): the stacked bridge \(q_{\text{SWL}}\) is the proposal, the per-PV likelihood ratio \(L_m^0(\beta_s)/q_{\text{SWL}}(\beta_s)\) gives the weights, and PSIS stabilizes the tails. So the rigorous form of “use importance resampling” is already implemented. The real question is whether to extend it to the variance-component directions — and here the heavy-tail risk you raised in C1 bites directly. The bridge is, by Theorem 2.2, a good proposal for \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) (its mode sits at \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\), where the per-PV posteriors concentrate). For \(\sigma^2_{\text{school}}\) there is no such alignment guarantee: in a high-ICC setting (Korea’s mathematics ICC \(\approx 0.45\); the reading data analyzed in Section 12.1 is milder) the marginal can be sharply peaked with a heavy right tail, and the all-PV bridge can misplace its mass relative to a single PV’s posterior — exactly the high-\(\hat k\), unreliable-PSIS regime. So extending joint IS to the VC directions would walk into the tail problem without the mode-alignment that keeps \(\hat k\) manageable in the \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) directions.

The resulting strategy is therefore not a restriction of joint IS but the joint strategy with each block given its correct treatment:

  • \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\): deterministic CCC by default (A, C-Direct); Pipeline B adds PSIS resampling in these directions with a Pareto-\(\hat k\) gate — preferred when \(\hat k_m<0.7\), falling back to C-Direct otherwise.
  • \((\sigma^2_{\text{school}},\sigma^2)\): identity passthrough here; design-based VC calibration, including the joint-IS question, is the follow-up paper.

6.7 Weighted CCC for Pipeline B

When Pipeline B supplies normalized PSIS weights \(\{\tilde w_{m,s}\}\) (\(\sum_s\tilde w_{m,s}=1\)), the raw moments become their weighted counterparts, \[ \bar\beta^{\text{raw}} = \sum_s \tilde w_{m,s}\,\beta_s, \qquad \Sigma_{\text{raw}} = \sum_s \tilde w_{m,s}\,(\beta_s-\bar\beta^{\text{raw}})(\beta_s-\bar\beta^{\text{raw}})^\top, \] the self-normalized weighted covariance the engine actually computes (the weights sum to one; the engine uses this convention deliberately rather than a Bessel-corrected estimator, the difference being \(O(\sum_s\tilde w_{m,s}^2)\) — negligible at the draw counts used). Should \(\Sigma_{\text{raw}}\) come out numerically non-positive-definite, a conditional nearest-PD repair (an eigenvalue floor at \(\varepsilon\cdot\operatorname{tr}(\Sigma_{\text{raw}})/p\) with \(\varepsilon = 10^{-10}\)) is applied before the Cholesky — it fires only when needed, not as an unconditional ridge. The per-draw map is unchanged; only the ingredients of \(A\) reflect the reweighting. Unweighted CCC (A, C-Direct) is the special case \(\tilde w_{m,s}=1/S\). In code a single draw_weights argument to engine_ccc() covers both (Section 8.1).

6.8 Summary

CCC is deterministic, non-iterative, and exact on \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) in the moment sense — mean \(c\) and covariance \(\Sigma_{\text{target}}\) to machine precision — though not a full distributional correction beyond Gaussianity. The restriction to \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) compounds three justifications: Theorem 2.2 gives an exact anchor only there; no design-based VC target is built in this paper; and the affine map is structurally unsafe on positivity-constrained parameters. Joint importance resampling is available as Pipeline B for the \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) directions, with a Pareto-\(\hat k\) diagnostic (Section 7.1); the variance-component directions are the follow-up paper. The code is ccc_twolevel() / engine_ccc() (Section 8.1).
