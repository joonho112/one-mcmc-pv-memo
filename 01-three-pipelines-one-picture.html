1  The Three Pipelines in One Picture

PISA 2022 does not release a single achievement score per student. It releases \(M = 10\) plausible values (PVs) — ten draws from each student’s posterior ability distribution, generated by the consortium’s IRT model before the data file ever reaches you — so that a downstream analyst can propagate measurement uncertainty into any statistic. For a survey-weighted two-level random-intercept regression (an Allison hybrid with within-school slopes \(\beta^W\), between-school slopes \(\beta^B\), school effects \(\zeta_j\), and PISA’s 80 BRR-Fay replicate weights), the orthodox recipe is exactly what you would expect: fit the model ten times, once per PV, then pool the ten coefficient vectors and their variances by Rubin’s (1987) rules. That is Pipeline A — correct, defensible, and the reference against which everything in this book is judged.

The proposal is that you can obtain the same Rubin-style report — the same point estimate and the same total variance — from one MCMC run instead of ten. That is the claim of Pipelines B and C. Before any theorems, I want you to see why all three procedures are variations on a single skeleton, differing on only two axes.

1.1 The shared spine

The three pipelines have far more in common than not. They share a point center, a variance target, and a reshaping operator.

A single point center. Every pipeline reports the Rubin-pooled fixed-effect estimate \[ \bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\hat\beta_m, \qquad \hat\beta_m = (X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega\, y^{(m)}, \] where \(\hat\beta_m\) is the per-PV GLS estimate and \(\Omega = V^{-1}\) is the marginal precision at plug-in variance components.

A single variance target. Every pipeline reports the Rubin total variance \[ T_{\text{MI}} = \bar U + \Bigl(1 + \tfrac{1}{M}\Bigr) B, \] where \(\bar U = M^{-1}\sum_m \hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\) is the average within-PV design variance (assembled from the \(R = 80\) replicate weights with Fay shrinkage \(k = 0.5\)) and \(B = (M-1)^{-1}\sum_m(\hat\beta_m - \bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}})(\hat\beta_m - \bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}})^\top\) is the between-PV parameter scatter. Exactly how \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is assembled — and why it is the crux of your question — is Section 5.1.

A single reshaping operator. Raw MCMC draws have the right center but the wrong covariance: the sampler’s implied uncertainty, not PISA’s design-based uncertainty. The Cholesky Calibration Correction (CCC) maps a raw draw cloud to a prescribed mean \(c\) and covariance \(\Sigma_{\text{target}}\): \[ \beta_s^{\text{cal}} = c + L_{\text{target}}\,L_{\text{raw}}^{-1}\bigl(\beta_s - \bar\beta^{\text{raw}}\bigr), \qquad A = L_{\text{target}}L_{\text{raw}}^{-1}, \] with \(L_{\text{raw}}, L_{\text{target}}\) the lower-triangular Cholesky factors of the raw and target covariances. CCC is deterministic and closed-form — it adds no sampling noise — and it acts only on the fixed-effect block \(\beta_{\text{FE}} = (\beta^W,\beta^B)^\top\); variance components pass through untouched. The reason, which turns out to be the heart of your “why only the conditional version” question, is that GLS fixed-effect gradients are linear in \(y^{(m)}\) while variance-component scores are not (Section 6.1).

1.2 The two axes of divergence

Given that spine, the three pipelines differ on exactly two choices — and nothing else.

Axis 1 — where the raw draws come from. Pipeline A runs \(M = 10\) independent MCMC chains, one per PV. Pipelines B and C share a single MCMC run from the stacked fractional bridge \[ \log q_{\text{SWL}}(\psi) = \log p(\psi) + \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\log L_m^0(\psi) + C, \] a geometric-mean blend of the ten per-PV likelihoods. Theorem 2.2 (Chapter 3) shows that the mode of \(q_{\text{SWL}}\) over \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\) equals \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) exactly — under the R3a plug-in regime, where a single pilot \(\hat V\) is held fixed. The real joint MCMC operates under R3b (variance components sampled jointly), which opens a small gap we measure rather than assume away (Chapters 3 and 10).

Axis 2 — at what granularity CCC is applied. Pipelines A and B call CCC \(M\) times, once per PV, each call targeting the per-PV BRR-Fay sandwich \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\); the pooled \(T_{\text{MI}}\) then emerges afterward as the Rubin combination of those calibrated clouds. Pipeline C-Direct calls CCC exactly once, with center \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) and target \(T_{\text{MI}}\) supplied directly. The one-shot shortcut is licensed by Theorem 2.2: because the stacked bridge already centers its draws at \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\), a single CCC pass against \(T_{\text{MI}}\) reproduces what the ten-call-then-pool procedure produces.

One caution the picture makes visible: injecting \(T_{\text{MI}}\) as a per-PV target in A or B would be wrong — it would over-disperse each individual cloud by the between-PV term \((1+1/M)B\). The pooled target is a valid direct CCC input only for C-Direct’s single cloud.

All three lanes feed the same CCC operator and emerge at the same \((\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}},\, T_{\text{MI}})\) output. The BRR-Fay subroutine sits in the middle, supplying target material to every lane regardless of which one you choose.

Theorem 2.2’s mode identity — the raw stacked posterior mean equalling \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) — is exact only under the R3a plug-in regime (a single pilot \(\hat V\) held fixed); the deployed R3b MCMC (variance components sampled jointly) opens a small gap between that raw mean and \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\). That gap never enters C-Direct’s report, which is centered on the external \(\bar\beta_{\text{Rubin}}\) — so its point estimate equals Pipeline A’s exactly, by construction. We measure the raw-mean gap only as a verification diagnostic — see Chapter 3 and the known-truth simulation in Part IV.

Two things the table hides. First, Pipeline B is not simply Pipeline A with a gate bolted on: it reweights the one shared draw cloud toward each per-PV posterior using PSIS importance weights, and only then calibrates. “PSIS needed” means each of the ten per-PV weight sets is checked by a Pareto \(\hat k\) diagnostic; any \(\hat k \ge 0.7\) triggers a fallback (to Pipeline A for that PV, or to C-Direct) — this is exactly the tail concern you raised, and Section 7.1 develops it. Second, and I want to be explicit: Pipeline C does not escape the BRR-Fay cost.

1.5 Where the cost actually lives

If Pipeline C needs only one MCMC run, where did the work go? It went into the variance target — and that work is shared by all three pipelines. Assembling \(\hat U_m^{\text{BRR-Fay}}\) and \(T_{\text{MI}}\) requires \(M(R+1) = 10 \times 81 = 810\) refits per country: one full-weight fit and \(R = 80\) replicate-weight fits for each of the ten PVs. Crucially these are fast lme4 REML fits, not MCMC — measured at roughly \(0.01\)–\(0.05\) s each at PISA scale (Section 5.1), so seconds to a few minutes per country (a pessimistic upper bound of $$15–30 minutes with a rich covariate set), against the roughly two-to-four hours that ten full MCMC fits demand (USA to Korea, measured at PISA scale in Section 13.1).

So Pipeline C does not make the variance target free, and it does not make it pipeline-specific: the 810 fits are model-specific and unavoidable, and every pipeline pays exactly the same price for them. What Pipeline C changes is one number only — the MCMC count, from ten to one. That is the whole of the efficiency claim, stated honestly. Your comment C4 asks where this target comes from and whether it secretly needs many model fits; the honest answer is “yes, many — but cheap ones, and shared,” and Section 5.1 makes it concrete.

1.6 What this book does

The remaining chapters develop this single picture from four angles:

  1. Mathematics — the stacked MLE identity (Theorem 2.2) that licenses the point estimate; the translation from plausible-value variance to parameter variance; the BRR-Fay assembly of \(T_{\text{MI}}\); and the CCC algebra, including why it touches only \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\).
  2. Code — five R functions in one-to-one correspondence with the five objects in the diagram above (stack_fractional_posterior, assemble_pisa_brr_fay_target, rubin_pool_matrix, ccc_twolevel, and the pipeline runners).
  3. A known-truth simulation — synthetic PVs with a known generating \(\beta\), where we can watch Pipeline C recover the truth and reproduce Pipeline A to within Monte-Carlo error.
  4. PISA 2022 — the three pipelines run side by side for the USA and Korea.

The next chapter restates your five comments verbatim and points each one to the chapter that answers it (and Section 14.1 closes them out at the very end). If you want to go straight to “where does \(T_{\text{MI}}\) come from without running A or B,” that is Section 5.1; if you want the stacked MCMC and the importance-sampling step, that is Section 7.1. This chapter’s only job was to put all three pipelines in one frame so the arguments that follow have somewhere to land.
