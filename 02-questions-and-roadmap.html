2  Your Questions, Restated — and a Roadmap

You left five comments on the proposal and one summarizing paragraph in your email. They are the best possible table of contents for this book, because each one points at a place where the proposal was too terse. This chapter quotes them back to you verbatim, says what I now understand each to be asking, and names the chapter that answers it. Nothing here is an answer yet — it is a map.

Your email put the central difficulty plainly:

The first two approaches seem solid. I’m not able to follow the third approach. I’m not following where the external total variance \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is coming from. My best guess is that it is coming from the plausible values \(y\) for the between and within. But I missed the connection to the variance of the regression parameters (Betas).

That single paragraph actually contains two distinct questions — where does \(T_{\text{MI}}\) come from? and how does a variance over plausible values become a variance over \(\beta\)? — and they are answered in two different chapters. Let me take all five margin comments in the order that builds.

2.1 The central one: where does the variance target come from? (C2, C4)

C4. I don’t understand where this comes from? isn’t it model specific and needs to be computed by estimating multiple models?

C2. How do we get this without using A or B methods?

This is the heart of the matter, and your instinct is correct: \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is model-specific, and it does require fitting many models. The proposal hid that, which is what made Pipeline C look like it was conjuring a variance from nowhere. The clarification is about which kind of fit. The expensive object in this whole problem is the MCMC: Pipeline A runs ten of them, Pipeline C runs one. The variance target is a separate object, assembled from \(M(R{+}1) = 810\) fast lme4 REML replicate refits — seconds each, not hours — and that assembly is identical and shared across Pipelines A, B, and C. So \(T_{\text{MI}}\) is not gotten “without A or B”; it is gotten by the same BRR-Fay machinery all three share, which is cheap and has nothing to do with how many MCMC runs you did. Pipeline C’s only economy is the MCMC count. This is Section 5.1, the chapter I most want you to read.

2.2 The connecting one: plausible-value variance vs. parameter variance (C3)

C3. Isn’t this for the plausible values \(y\) not the model parameters (betas?) How do we connect this to the variance of the parameters?

Exactly the right question, and the proposal never drew the arrow. The between-imputation term \((1+1/M)B\) is not a variance of the plausible values \(y\). Each plausible value \(y^{(m)}\) is pushed through the regression to a coefficient vector \(\hat\beta_m = (X^\top\Omega X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega\, y^{(m)}\), and \(B\) is the scatter of those ten coefficient vectors in parameter space. The variability lives in \(y\) but is measured in \(\beta\), through the linear GLS map. Section 4.1 draws that arrow carefully, with the worked arithmetic.

2.3 The subtle one: why calibrate only the betas? (C5)

C5. fixing the variance components? why not do importance resampling for the joint distribution instead of the conditional/profile version?

Two good ideas are bundled here. First, why is the calibration restricted to the fixed effects, holding the variance components fixed? Because the licensing identity (Theorem 2.2) is exact only for \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\): the GLS fixed-effect gradient is linear in \(y^{(m)}\), while the variance-component scores are not, so the clean affine reshape has no analogue there — and the positivity constraint on \(\sigma^2\) would be violated by an affine map anyway. Second, why not importance-resample the joint distribution instead? You can — and that is exactly what Pipeline B does for the fixed-effect block, via PSIS. So C5 is not really an objection to Pipeline C so much as a description of the B-vs-C trade-off. Section 6.1 handles the “why only betas” half; Section 7.1 handles the “joint resampling” half. Calibrating the variance components themselves is a genuinely separate problem, and we defer it to the follow-up paper.

2.4 The generative one: making importance sampling work (C1)

C1. This seems like a good starting point, esp for the point estimates. For importance sampling we may want to further fit a second time and further temper to get better proposal values that are more likely to include the modes from the replicate distributions. Should double check the pareto version of importance sampling.

And from your email:

I think we might need higher multiples of \(M\) (say \(1/2M\) or \(1/3M\)) to flatten out the proposal distribution for the importance sampling. Each of the \(M\) target distributions could be shifted away from the overall mode. With larger sample sizes, the mode could be islands with less overlap … But too much tempering could skew the point estimates, hence wanting to run it for the \(1/M\) first.

This is not a question I need to defend against — it is a design idea I want to build with you. The stacked bridge is a good point-estimate proposal (Theorem 2.2 makes its mode exact), but it can be a poor importance-sampling proposal when the per-PV posteriors separate into the “islands” you describe. The Pareto \(\hat k\) diagnostic is our primary instrument for detecting this, PV by PV — and, taking your “double check the pareto version” seriously, whether it is sufficient once the modes separate is something we test in Section 11.1, not something we assume. Your tempering suggestion — raising the exponent to \(1/(2M)\) or \(1/(3M)\) to flatten the proposal — is a real fix, already reachable through one knob in the code. Section 7.1 lays out the geometry, and Section 11.1 actually runs your \(1/M\) vs \(1/2M\) vs \(1/3M\) experiment on data with a known answer, so we can see the trade-off you anticipated between proposal coverage and point-estimate stability.

2.5 The roadmap

The Preface holds the same five comments as a one-screen table. In chapter terms:

  • Section 5.1 — where \(T_{\text{MI}}\) comes from; the cheap-refit answer to C2, C4.
  • Section 4.1 — plausible-value variance → parameter variance; C3.
  • Section 6.1 — the CCC operator and why only \(\beta_{\text{FE}}\); C5 (first half).
  • Section 7.1 + Section 11.1 — importance sampling, the \(\hat k\) diagnostic, and your tempering idea, demonstrated; C1 and C5 (second half).

Those are the mathematics and demonstrations. Read them in any order — each is self-contained — and Section 14.1 collects all five comments at the end with the answers in hand, so we can check them off together.
